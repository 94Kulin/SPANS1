Measurement Principles, Formulas & References

What you’re measuring
  • Emission mask (near in-band & OOB): the modulated spectrum must sit under a mask defined by the standard, for adjacent-channel protection.
  • Spurious emissions (far-out): unintended emissions outside the mask domain; checked against absolute limits / attenuation rules.
Rules & formulas

ETSI EN 302 217-2 V3.4.1 — Mask measurement settings

  • Span (sweep width): if CS < 500 MHz ⇒ SPAN ≥ 5×CS; if CS ≥ 500 MHz ⇒ SPAN ≥ 3×CS + 1000 MHz. (Table 7, § 5.2.3)
  • RBW (IF bandwidth): use the RBW step for your CS from Table 7 (typical steps 1/3/10/30/100/300 kHz, then 2 MHz/5 MHz at very large CS). (Table 7, § 5.2.3)
  • VBW: per Table 7, paired with RBW (e.g., 0.003/0.01/0.03/0.1/0.3 kHz … then 3/5 kHz for large CS). (Table 7, § 5.2.3)
  • Mask extent: masks extend to 2.5×CS (or 1.5×CS + 500 MHz when emission bandwidths exceed 500 MHz). (§ 4.2.3 & figs 3–6)
  • Discrete CW lines over mask: aggregation allowance based on IF bandwidth and CSmin (formulas (1) & (2)). (§ 4.2.4.2)

FCC 47 CFR § 101.111 — Emission limitations (digital)

  • Mask region (50–250% offset): evaluate attenuation in the stated reference bandwidth: below 15 GHz “in any 4 kHz band”; above 15 GHz “in any 1 MHz band.” (§ 101.111(a)(2)(i–ii))
  • Far-out (>250% offset): at least 43 + 10·log10(P[W]) dB (or 80 dB, whichever is less); minimum absolute floor −13 dBm/1 MHz appears in notes to (i–ii). (§ 101.111(a)(2)(iii))
  • Span: not prescribed by FCC; common practice is SPAN ≈ 5×B for OOB visualization; document chosen span.
  • RBW quick rule (spurious scans): based on center frequency fc: if fc ≤ 1 GHz use 100 kHz; if fc > 1 GHz use 1 MHz. (industry practice consistent with § 101.111 ref-BWs)
What changes when you change a setting

RBW (IF bandwidth)

  • Noise floor: approximately −174 dBm/Hz + 10·log10(RBW) + NF. Narrow RBW lowers displayed noise (better dynamic range), wider RBW raises noise (faster sweep).
  • Too wide hides narrow lines and over-integrates noise; too narrow makes the trace noisy/slow and may miss short peaks.
  • Compliance: for ETSI masks use Table 7 RBW (that’s what the mask assumes). For FCC equations, respect the stated reference bandwidth (4 kHz or 1 MHz).

VBW (video bandwidth)

  • Trace stability: VBW is a post-detector low-pass. VBW ≪ RBW smooths noise-like shoulders (stable average). VBW = RBW shows true peaks (more flicker).
  • If you measure noise-like shoulders with a wide VBW or peak detector, the trace flickers and shows occasional spikes that are not representative of average power.
  • ETSI: follow Table 7 pairings. FCC: for peak compliance readings, VBW = RBW is typical; if you use averaging for diagnostics, note VBW explicitly.

Span (sweep width)

  • Too small → you won’t see the full mask extent / spurious boundary; too large → long sweep, risk of missing peaks unless RBW is adjusted.
  • ETSI: SPAN ≥ 5×CS (CS<500) or ≥ 3×CS + 1000 MHz (CS≥500); high-band tests may be segmented. (Table 7)
  • FCC: choose span to cover 50–250% offsets (mask region) and >250% (far-out); common practice ≈ 5×B; document your choice.

Detector & trace

  • Peak + Max Hold: captures worst-case spikes; essential mindset for emission masks.
  • RMS/Average: shows average of noise-like shoulders; pair with VBW ≪ RBW to stabilize; if you report averages, document the detector/VBW.
  • Sample: fastest but can under-read peaks on noise-like spectra.

Reference level, attenuation, preamp

  • Reference Level: set to approx. the TX power at the analyzer input (after external attenuation) so the front-end stays linear.
  • Reference Level Offset: add cables/attenuator/notch loss as a positive offset so on-screen levels equal at-port levels.
  • Input Attenuation: increase until changing attenuation does not change measured spur levels → confirms no overload.
  • Preamplifier: use only when the carrier is removed/notched and you are noise-limited; preamps can overload on strong carriers and create false spurs.

Averaging & sweep time

  • Trace averaging (VBW or sweep averaging) reduces variance on noise-like spectra, improving repeatability but possibly hiding short peaks.
  • Sweep time: with narrow RBW or large spans, use auto or longer sweeps to ensure peak capture.

Normalization & bandwidth units

  • Check whether limits are dBm, dBm/MHz, or dBc. If your measurement RBW ≠ 1 MHz, normalize power by 10·log10(1 MHz/RBW) to compare to per-MHz limits.
  • ETSI masks are spectral density masks linked to CS; FCC equations define attenuation vs percent offset and specify the reference bandwidth directly.
Where to look (standard sections)
  • ETSI EN 302 217-2 V3.4.1: Spectrum analyzer settings (Span/RBW/VBW): § 5.2.3, Table 7; Mask shapes & extent 2.5×CS: § 4.2.3 & figs 3–6; Discrete CW aggregation: § 4.2.4.2; Spurious domain reference: § 4.2.5.
  • FCC § 101.111: Digital mask equations + reference bandwidths (4 kHz & 1 MHz): § 101.111(a)(2)(i–ii); Far-out rule (>250%): § 101.111(a)(2)(iii).
Practical quick-start
  1. Protect analyzer (fixed pad), set RefLvl ≈ TX at input; set RefLvl Offset to external loss.
  2. Select Standard & CS/BW.
  3. ETSI mask: set Span/RBW/VBW from Table 7; Peak + Max Hold; check K/f mask points.
  4. FCC mask: use § 101.111 equations; below 15 GHz measure “in any 4 kHz band”, above 15 GHz “in any 1 MHz band.”
  5. Spurious scan: extend to spurious domain; RBW per reference bandwidth; maintain enough attenuation to avoid overload; normalize if limits are per-MHz.
  6. If noise-like shoulders flicker, reduce VBW (≤ ~0.3×RBW) or use RMS detector for diagnostics; report peak where required.
